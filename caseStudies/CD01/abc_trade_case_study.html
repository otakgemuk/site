Annotated Chart
01
ABC Correction Pattern

From E30 to E44, the market traces a classic ABC correction within what remains a bullish higher-timeframe context. The A-leg pushes down from the swing high, the B-leg delivers a partial retracement, and the C-leg retests and slightly undercuts the A-leg low.

Bar E44 is the defining moment: it forms a breakout bar below the A-leg low, then closes well off its low — creating a 2-bar reversal with E43. This is the market showing its hand. The bears pushed hard and failed to hold.

The buy trigger: one tick above the high of E44.

ABC correction 2-bar reversal breakout failure
02
Entry, Fill & TBTL

The order filled at E46 — two bars after the signal. The stop is placed one tick below E44 initially, then tightened to one tick below E45 once that bar closes as a bullish bar.

Bars E47 through E53 form a TBTL — Ten Bar Two Legs — sideways consolidation. This is normal and expected after a reversal. Al Brooks notes this pattern repeatedly: the market often pauses in a tight range before confirming the new direction.

Patience is critical here. Nothing about the TBTL structure invalidates the original setup. The stop remains untested throughout.

TBTL stop management continuation
03
High 2 Confirmation & Exit

After the TBTL concludes, the market makes two small legs down within the consolidation. Bar E55 then forms a strong bullish signal bar at the low of the range — this is the High 2 setup. Buying one tick above E56 provides a second entry for those who missed E46, and adds to existing positions for those already long.

The measured move is simple: take the range of the E44 bar (from low to high), project it upward from the E44 high. This gives the 1:1 target, reached precisely at E60.

Exit at E60 yields exactly 12.00 points.

Key Concepts
ABC Correction Pattern
A three-wave corrective structure within a trend. The A-wave is the initial impulse against the trend, B is a partial retracement back in trend direction, and C is a second push against the trend — typically ending near or slightly below the A-wave low. When the C-leg fails to extend (breakout failure), the original trend is likely to resume.
TBTL — Ten Bar Two Legs
Al Brooks' observation that after a reversal, the market frequently consolidates for approximately ten bars in a two-legged sideways range before continuing. Traders who exit during TBTL often re-enter at worse prices. Staying in a position through TBTL — provided the stop is not hit — is generally the correct strategy when the original setup remains valid.
High 2 Setup
A two-legged pullback within a bull trend or bull rally, where the second pullback low holds above the first pullback low. The "High 2" label refers to it being the second attempt by bears to push price down — and failing. Buying above the signal bar at the end of the second pullback leg is a reliable trend-continuation entry in Al Brooks methodology.
1:1 Measured Move
A price target derived by measuring the height of a key bar or swing and projecting that distance forward in the direction of the trade. In this case, the range of E44 (from its low to its high) was projected upward from the E44 high. This gives a minimum target expectation. Al Brooks considers the 1:1 measured move to be the most reliable target for continuation trades.
Lessons

Key takeaways
01
Structure first, entry second. The ABC pattern provided the structural context. The 2-bar reversal at E44 provided the trigger. Neither alone is sufficient — the combination of context plus signal is what makes the trade high-probability.
02
TBTL is not a reason to exit. The ten-bar consolidation (E47–E53) tested patience but never invalidated the structure. Exiting during TBTL would have meant re-entering at worse prices. Trust the setup until the stop is hit.
03
Second entries offer lower risk. The High 2 at E55/E56 gave traders who missed E46 a second chance with a tighter, well-defined stop. Second entries after TBTL consolidations are often superior risk/reward compared to the original entry.
04
Measured moves provide objective exits. Defining the 1:1 target before entry removes emotion from the exit decision. Price reached the exact 1:1 level at E60. Having a predetermined exit prevents both premature exits and overstaying a trade.
05
Stop management is active, not passive. Moving the stop from below E44 to below E45 reduced risk by roughly 30% without changing the trade thesis. Active stop management is part of the trade — not an afterthought.
